Antonio Paucar

Share

https://durresbiennale.org/en/artists/Peru/artists/Antonio-Paucar

Antonio PAUCAR was born in 1973 in Huancayo, Peru. He graduated in Visual Arts at the Universität der Künste, Berlin. Paucar creates an entirely personal artistic language through performances, sculptures and video art. His artistic practice delves into the themes of contemporary conflict, the assassination of indigenous leaders, climate change and surveillance technology. Inspired by ancestral traditions from the Peruvian Andes and the Amazon, Paucar raises profound questions about the consequences of colonization in the present. The artist fosters a dialogue between different world art scenes, enriching his work with a multifaceted perspective. His artistic creativity has been exhibited in many important institutions in: Peru, Germany, Mexico, Philippines, Spain, Japan and the United States of America. His pieces are part of international collections, including the Museum of Modern Art in New York, the Museo de Arte de Lima and the Institut für Auslandsbeziehungen, Berlin. Paucar has been awarded the LARA Prize (2013) and the Art Prize Kunstpreis Zeitsicht (2011), Augsburg. He lives and works between Berlin and Huancayo in Peru.


Waiting for a ChangeVideo-art, 1:27 min, 2005


  • The conceptual approach:


    Antonio Paucar's video art work “Waiting for a Change” (2005) captures the artist standing on his head in front of a traffic light on a street in West Berlin. At a busy crosswalk. Paucar remains upside down, resting only on his head, his arms tucked behind his body, reflecting the distinctive image of a red pedestrian light at a traffic light. The video juxtaposes the meditative calm of his pose with the dynamic urban landscape, highlighting the artist's unusual calm amid the city's rush and the noise of car traffic.

    While Paucar stands upside down for the duration of the red light (over a minute), pedestrians cross the road, unaware of his stability and the signal to rest. This performance transforms the ordinary act of waiting at traffic lights into a profound meditation on patience, waiting for change and perspective. When the light turns green, Paucar descends from his plinth, crosses the street, and exits the frame, embodying a literal figurative and mood shift. The work challenges viewers to reconsider the rhythms of everyday life, mixing physical endurance with a contemplative urban presence. The Burnout Society, as the South Korean philosopher Byung Chul Han calls it, after the video, although it was made in 2005, its message remains valid even today, inviting us to ask ourselves if we expect change or destruction?!
