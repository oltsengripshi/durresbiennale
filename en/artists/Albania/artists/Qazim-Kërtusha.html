Qazim Kërtusha

Share

https://durresbiennale.org/en/artists/Albania/artists/Qazim-Kërtusha

Qazim KËRTUSHA was born in Durrës. After initially attending the sculpture course at Sabri Tuçi's studio, Kërtusha continued her higher studies at the Academy of Fine Arts in Tirana for monumental sculpture, where she had the opportunity to be guided by some of the most prominent names in Albanian sculpture, such as: Kristaq Rama, Muntaz Dhrami, Hektor Dule and Thoma Thomai.

After finishing his higher studies in 1975, he started working as a restorer at the Archaeological Museum of Durrës, which gave him the opportunity to understand more deeply how cultural stratifications are created, serving him later in his creativity.
The sculptor Kërtusha is the author of many solo exhibitions in: Venetian Tower, Durrës (1997), Palace of Culture, Durrës (2000); Former House of Officers, Durrës (2006); Joseph Gallery, Durrës (2012); Nikolet Vasia Fine Arts Gallery, Durrës (2014); Archaeological Museum, Durrës (2017) and FAB Gallery - Faculty of Fine Arts, Tirana (2022).

He has also participated in many joint exhibitions in: National Art Gallery, Tirana (1993); Margherita Theater Museum, Bari (1994); C.I.C.A.L, Brindisi (1997); Swabian Norman Castle, Mesagne (2011); Edwart Lear Gallery, Berat (2016); Pop Up Space, New York (2018); Residenza D'Italia, Tirana (2019) and the Archaeological Museum, Durrës (2021).


The power of money
Wood, 350 cm x 30 x 40 cm, 2017


  • The conceptual approach:


    This work, from the way it was solved, also finds a place in urban spaces, parks and squares. By using the vertical volume fragmented into blocks, which stands next to the verticality of the building, its monumentality increases in progression. The work is a column in the shape of a parallelepiped carved in wood and toned in dark wenge color. The obelisk (column) consists of 6 parallelopiped-shaped drums of different sizes, where it sometimes narrows and sometimes expands, being closed in the upper part with a U-shaped plastic volume. All volumes and shapes are attached to each other by sticking to the vertical. The obelisk (column) consists of 6 volumes, on which various motifs are engraved, from the symbolic element of the keyhole in the door lock, to the hinge and the male-female nut-bolt binomial.
