Michele Stanzione

Share

https://durresbiennale.org/en/artists/Italy/artists/Michele-Stanzione

Michele Stanzione was born in 1981 in Benevento, Italy. He is an Italian photographer who has always been fascinated by the expressive power and communication of artistic photography. Stanzione began his artistic journey as a photographer, creating a series of surrealist photographs in which the use of monochrome, black and white prevailed. His artistic maturity continued with his departure from surrealism towards a stronger realism, that of reportage photography. Another fundamental phase of his career was marked by the magnificent landscapes of the war-torn lands of Vietnam, Laos and Cambodia. The attention of his target was focused on the depth of the views of those populations. His photographs were purchased by National Geographic (USA).


Entrapment
Artistic photography, 500 x 70 cm, 2024


  • The conceptual approach


    Pictures of women wrapped in a sheet bring a strong emotion, a silent cry of imprisonment and despair. These images are not only an artistic expression, but a powerful metaphor for our deepest fears and struggles. Imagine a woman wrapped in a thin veil, which presses her body like a second skin, restricting her movements, suffocating her essence. Every contour, every fold of the garment, tells a story of coercion, of struggle against an invisible and indomitable force. This vision evokes a reality many of us know all too well: the feeling of being trapped, limited by something bigger than us, something we cannot control. In a world dominated by artificial intelligence, this fabric prison perfectly represents our condition. Artificial intelligence, with its omnipresent presence and its ability to influence every aspect of our lives, can seem like a force that wraps us, that holds us, like that fabric wraps around a woman. We find ourselves faced with algorithms that make decisions for us, with machines that analyze and predict our every move, with an invisible web of data that captures and holds us. Ultimately, these images speak to us of hope and resistance. They invite us to recognize our fears, but also to rejoice in our ability to overcome them. The woman trapped in the garment is not only a symbol of coercion, but also of an inner strength that does not give up, that continues to fight for her freedom, for her humanity.
